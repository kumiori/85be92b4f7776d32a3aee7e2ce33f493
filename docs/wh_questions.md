WHO

Who is this event speaking to, and who is invited to take part?

This event addresses a mixed public: scientists, decision makers, cultural actors, journalists, students, and citizens who are concerned by climate change but engage with it in different ways. It is designed precisely for this diversity.

The intention is not to separate experts from non-experts, but to create a shared space where scientific language, public perception, and lived emotion can meet. Scientists are present not only to transmit knowledge, but also to listen, to test how their words resonate, and to draw energy from this exchange to sustain long-term engagement.

---

WHAT

What is the core proposition of the event?

The event explores three key concepts used by scientists to describe climate dynamics in the cryosphere: **stability, bifurcation, and irreversibility**.

Rather than explaining these terms in a purely technical way, the event investigates how they are _received_, understood, and felt by different publics. Irreversibility is approached not as a depressive or paralysing notion, but as something that leaves a trace and can, paradoxically, be empowering by clarifying what is at stake.

The event proposes a combination of short talks, participatory moments, and sensory experiences to create a meaningful connection between knowledge, perception, and action.

---

WHERE

Where does this take place, and why does the setting matter?

The event takes place at UNESCO, during the World Day for Glaciers. This setting is central.

UNESCO embodies the link between science, culture, education, and shared heritage. Glaciers, and in particular Antarctica, are not only physical systems but global commons whose fate concerns humanity as a whole. Hosting the event at UNESCO situates the discussion at this global, collective level.

---

WHEN

Why is this the right moment?

We are living through a phase where many cryospheric processes are crossing thresholds that cannot be reversed on human timescales. At the same time, public discourse often oscillates between alarm and disengagement.

This event takes place at a moment when it is crucial to transform awareness into sustained commitment. It explicitly connects to the **Decade of Action for Cryospheric Sciences (DACS 2025–2034)**, aiming to carry forward the momentum of the International Year into a long-term collective effort.

---

WHY

Why focus on connection, emotion, and participation?

Because understanding alone is not enough.

Scientific analysis is essential, but it does not automatically translate into engagement or action. Emotional reactions—fear, anxiety, excitement, hope—are often treated as obstacles to rational thinking, when in fact they can be powerful drivers if properly acknowledged and articulated.

The event deliberately explores the relationship between emotional impulse and rational work, seeking effective ways to link the two rather than oppose them.

---

HOW

How does the event work in practice?

The event combines several formats:

- Short scientific and contextual inputs.
- A web-based platform where participants can react, express resonance or dissonance, and submit questions.
- Collective exchanges that allow different perspectives to surface.
- Sensory experiences, such as placing an ice cube in a glass of water and listening to it crack, to create an embodied understanding of physical processes like fracture and energy release.

These different modes of engagement are designed to accommodate varied sensitivities and ways of understanding.

---

WITH WHAT MECHANISMS

How are collective decisions and questions handled?

Questions submitted by participants are collected through the platform and placed in a shared queue. They are not immediately answered, but addressed, reframed, or traced through the discussion.

The event experiments with simple mechanisms of collective decision making, making visible how groups converge, diverge, or hesitate when confronted with complex issues. The goal is not to reach consensus at all costs, but to observe and understand how collective reasoning unfolds.

---

WITH WHAT OUTCOMES

What is expected to come out of this?

The immediate outcome is a strengthened sense of connection:

- between science and society,
- between emotion and knowledge,
- between individual concern and collective responsibility.

More broadly, the event aims to replenish motivation and shared purpose—both for scientists engaged in cryospheric research and for all those who wish to contribute to the Decade of Action for Cryospheric Sciences. It seeks to transform awareness into sustained commitment and collective action, without simplifying the complexity of the issues at hand.

WHEN
If not now?

Because irreversibility is a present condition.

Glacial systems are already crossing thresholds that cannot be undone on human timescales. At the same time, public discourse remains trapped between alarmism and paralysis. The gap between knowledge and agency is widening.

This event takes place at a moment when the question is no longer “what do we know?” but “how do we decide together when reversal is no longer an option?”

---

WHY
participation?

Information alone does not generate action.

The prevailing model assumes that better data leads to better decisions. Decades of climate governance suggest otherwise. What is missing is not awareness, but structures that make responsibility tangible and shared.

By inviting participants to make small, explicit decisions—and by tracing those decisions—the event explores how agency can be reconstructed at a collective level, without pretending to resolve the crisis.

Participation here is not performative. It is consequential within the limits of the experiment.

---

AFTER
What happens?

The immediate outcome is a structured dataset: a map of how a diverse group engaged with questions of irreversibility and agency.

This material will be synthesised and published as an editorial report. Not a manifesto, but an account of collective reasoning under constraint.

More importantly, the tool and the method are designed to be reusable. The event functions as a prototype for future participatory formats in science communication, governance, and public deliberation.

---

The process is accountable to its participants, who can see how their contributions are aggregated and interpreted.

It is also accountable to the institutions involved, by making visible not just outcomes but the reasoning paths that led there.

Finally, it is accountable to the public record. By publishing questions rather than declarations, it leaves space for scrutiny, disagreement, and continuation rather than closure.

---

IS DELIBERATELY NOT CLAIMED
What this event does not pretend to do

It does not claim to solve climate change.  
It does not claim to represent “the public”.  
It does not claim neutrality.

Instead, it makes a more modest and more demanding claim: that under conditions of irreversibility, the way we decide together matters as much as the decisions themselves.
